An Orkney dig uncovering Britain’s stone age culture
奥克尼挖掘揭示英国石器时代文化

2022-02-17 00:51:23

Archaeologists excavating the windswept Ness of Brodgar are unearthing a treasure trove of neolithic villages, tombs, weapons and mysterious religious artefacts, some to be displayed in a blockbuster exhibition

挖掘布罗德加狂风肆虐的考古人员正在发掘新石器时代村庄、坟墓、武器和神秘宗教文物的宝藏，其中一些将在一场轰动一时的展览中展出

I f you happen to imagine that there’s not much left to discover of Britain’s stone age, or that its relics consist of hard-to-love postholes and scraps of bones, then you need to find your way to Orkney, that scatter of islands off Scotland’s north-east coast. On the archipelago’s Mainland, out towards the windswept west coast with its wave-battered cliffs, you will come to the Ness of Brodgar, an isthmus separating a pair of sparkling lochs, one of saltwater and one of freshwater. Just before the way narrows you’ll see the Stones of Stenness rising up before you. This ancient stone circle’s monoliths were once more numerous, but they remain elegant and imposing. Like a gateway into a liminal world of theatricality and magic, they lead the eye to another, even larger neolithic monument beyond the isthmus, elevated in the landscape as if on a stage. This is the Ring of Brodgar, its sharply individuated stones like giant dancers arrested mid-step – as local legend, indeed, has it.

如果你碰巧想到英国石器时代的遗迹已经所剩无几，或者它的遗迹由难以辨认的柱孔和骨头碎片组成，那么你需要找到去奥克尼的路，那是苏格兰东北海岸附近分散的岛屿。在群岛的大陆上，朝着风吹日晒的西海岸，那里有波浪拍打的悬崖，你会来到布罗德加的尼斯，一个地峡，分隔着两个闪闪发光的湖泊，一个是咸水，一个是淡水。就在道路变窄之前，你会看到狭窄的石头在你面前升起。这个古老的石圈的巨石再次出现，但它们仍然优雅而壮观。它们就像一扇通往戏剧和魔法极限世界的大门，将人们的视线引向地峡之外的另一座更大的新石器时代纪念碑，在景观中像在舞台上一样高耸。这就是布罗德加的戒指，它的个性鲜明的石头像是在舞步中间被逮捕的巨大舞者——正如当地传说中所说的那样。

It’s between these two stone circles that archaeologist Nick Card and his team are excavating a huge settlement of neolithic stone buildings. The earliest date from about 3300BC, their walls and hearths crisply intact, their pots and stone tools in remarkable profusion, the whole bounded by six-metre-wide monumental walls. “You could continue for several lifetimes and not get to the bottom of it,” says the neatly white-bearded, laconic Card as we gaze out over the site, presently covered with tarpaulin to protect it from the winter storms. “Every year it never fails to produce something that astounds us.” After nearly two decades of digging, they have excavated only about 10% of its area, and about 5% of its volume. It goes deep: buildings are stacked on the ruins of older ones; the place was in use for 1,000 years. When summer comes, they’ll dig again. When the coverings come off each July, says Card’s colleague, Anne Mitchell, “down you go and you’re among the ghosts of the past”.

考古学家尼克·卡德（Nick Card）和他的团队正是在这两个石头圈之间挖掘一个巨大的新石器时代石头建筑定居点。最早可追溯到公元前3300年左右，它们的墙壁和壁炉完好无损，陶器和石器极其丰富，整个建筑被六米宽的纪念墙包围。“你可能会持续好几辈子，而不会深入到它的底部，”当我们凝视着现场时，这张整洁的白胡子简洁的卡片说。目前，现场覆盖着防水油布，以保护它免受冬季风暴的影响。“每年它都能生产出令我们震惊的产品。”经过近20年的挖掘，他们只挖掘了约10%的面积和约5%的体积。它很深：建筑物堆在旧建筑的废墟上；这个地方使用了1000年。当夏天来临时，他们会再次挖掘。卡德的同事安妮·米切尔（Anne Mitchell）说，每年7月，当被子揭下时，“你就会倒下，成为过去的幽灵之一”。

Many of the most intriguing finds from the Ness of Brodgar will be on show at the British Museum this month in a new exhibition, the World of Stonehenge, a survey of northern-European prehistory from the era of the hunter-gatherers to the early bronze age, using the long, complex history of Britain’s most celebrated neolithic monument as the story’s spine. The landscape around the ness reminds me of the barrow-studded downs around Stonehenge, in fact – both have the feel of a vast stage, ready to be used for ritual or performance. The earliest buildings here are older than Stonehenge, though. “For neolithic stone architecture, Orkney blows everywhere else out of the water,” says Neil Wilkin, curator of the British Museum show.

大英博物馆将于本月举办一个名为“巨石阵的世界”的新展览，展示布罗德加的许多最有趣的发现。该展览旨在调查从狩猎采集者时代到早期青铜时代的北欧史前史，英国最著名的新石器时代纪念碑的复杂历史作为故事的脊梁。尼斯公园周围的景观让我想起了巨石阵周围布满手推车的低洼地带，事实上，这两个地方都有一种巨大舞台的感觉，可以用来举行仪式或表演。不过，这里最早的建筑比巨石阵还要古老。大英博物馆展览馆长尼尔·威尔金说：“对于新石器时代的石头建筑来说，奥克尼把其他地方都吹出了水面。”。

Elsewhere in Europe – including the settlement at Durrington Walls in Wiltshire, now thought to be where the builders of Stonehenge lived – wood was the favoured construction material for neolithic domestic buildings. But in Orkney the inhabitants also built their houses from stone. (There were perhaps many wooden structures too, but stone architecture around here is so profuse that the search for postholes feels less urgent; there are few trees on Orkney now, but maybe more then, as well as a plentiful supply of driftwood.) It means the whole archipelago is dotted not just with incredibly well preserved neolithic tombs, neatly built out of the local flagstone, but also villages – construction of a scale and quality that would not be repeated until well into the medieval era, 4,000 years later. These include the famous Skara Brae, a coastal settlement revealed when a storm whipped away the great blanket of sand that had hidden it until 1850. But there are other, more ghostly places, too, like the unexcavated village on the eroding coastline of Sanday that Mark Edmonds, local resident and York University archaeology professor emeritus, describes at the start of his book, Orcadia. Orkney was not peripheral or cut off in the stone age; its surrounding waters were not barriers but pathways, to Ireland, to mainland Britain, and beyond. Neolithic axes made of jadeite, quarried in the Italian Alps, have been found in Scotland: the most beautiful example, on loan from the National Museum of Scotland, will be in the World of Stonehenge show. “It’s so simple and so exquisite,” says the exhibition’s assistant curator, Jennifer Wexler, “with a kind of modernist feel to it. And it just sparkles in the light.” Aesthetics were important in this world.

在欧洲其他地方——包括威尔特郡杜灵顿墙的定居点，现在被认为是巨石阵建造者居住的地方——木材是新石器时代住宅建筑最受欢迎的建筑材料。但在奥克尼，居民们也用石头建造房屋。（这里可能也有很多木结构，但附近的石头建筑非常丰富，因此寻找柱孔的工作就不那么紧迫了；奥克尼现在几乎没有树木，但可能比那时更多，还有大量的浮木供应。）这意味着整个群岛不仅点缀着保存完好的新石器时代陵墓，这些陵墓整齐地用当地的石板建造而成，而且还有村庄——其规模和质量直到4000年后的中世纪才得以重现。其中包括著名的斯卡拉布雷（Skara Brae），这是一个沿海定居点，在1850年前，一场风暴卷走了隐藏在那里的大片沙子。但也有其他更鬼魅的地方，比如桑迪海岸线上的一个未开凿的村庄，当地居民、约克大学考古学名誉教授马克·爱德蒙在他的书《奥卡迪亚》的开头描述了这个村庄。在石器时代，奥克尼并没有被边缘化或割断；它周围的水域不是屏障，而是通往爱尔兰、英国大陆和其他地方的通道。在意大利阿尔卑斯山采石的新石器时代硬玉斧头在苏格兰被发现：最美丽的例子是从苏格兰国家博物馆借来的巨石阵世界展。展览助理策展人珍妮弗·韦克斯勒说：“它如此简单、如此精致，带有一种现代主义的感觉。它在灯光下闪闪发光。”美学在这个世界上很重要。

The characteristic pottery – “grooved ware” – used by the builders of Stonehenge was first made in Orkney, it is thought. Card and his team have found 100,000 sherds of it so far. Mitchell explains how they tease it out of the middens (ancient refuse heaps) using a cake slice and an aluminium sheet salvaged from an old helicopter. A lot of these sherds are in boxes stacked floor-to-ceiling in their HQ, a bungalow on the site bursting with material. Some of the pottery is rather crumbly – like “digestive biscuits”, says ceramics expert Roy Towers, one of the volunteers on this donations-funded dig. Jan Blatchford, another highly skilled volunteer, shows me how reflectance transformation imaging – a digital photography technique using multiple camera angles – can expose details barely visible to the naked eye. Here is the mark where a plaited mat made its imprint on the base of one newly made, unfired pot; here are the whorls of the maker’s thumbprint on another, bringing the dizzyingly intimate sense of a human hand shaping the vessel 5,000 years ago.

人们认为，巨石阵建造者使用的特色陶器——“沟槽陶器”最早是在奥克尼制作的。到目前为止，卡德和他的团队已经发现了10万块碎片。米切尔解释了他们是如何用从一架旧直升机上打捞出来的蛋糕片和铝板从正午（古代垃圾堆）中挑出垃圾的。在他们的总部，很多碎片都被放在盒子里，从地板到天花板堆砌在一起。总部是一座装满材料的平房。陶艺专家罗伊·托尔斯（Roy Towers）说，有些陶器相当易碎，就像“消化饼干”，他是这项捐赠资助挖掘的志愿者之一。Jan Blatchford是另一位技术娴熟的志愿者，他向我展示了反射变换成像（Reflection transformation imaging）——一种使用多个相机角度的数字摄影技术——如何暴露肉眼几乎看不见的细节。这是一个标记，一个编结的垫子在一个新做的未烧制的罐子的底座上留下了印记；下面是制造者拇指指纹在另一个手指上的轮回，带来了5000年前人类手塑造容器的令人眩晕的亲密感。

It was out of the window of this little house that, washing vegetables at her sink one day in 2003, the former owner saw a plough stutter as a piece of masonry got lodged between its blades. It was worked, neolithic stone. The farmer contacted the local archaeologists. “We’d all been driving past the place for years assuming it was a natural ridge,” says Edmonds – rather than ancient ruins, hiding in plain sight. The immense settlement is an ongoing revelation – and yet, perhaps, not entirely surprising. The islands are teeming with neolithic remains. Edmonds tells me he once interviewed Orcadian families, asking them about the ancient stone axes and other finds they have on their mantelpieces, rich with stories of discovery by this or that great-grandparent. Some of these neolithic finds, like the intricately carved stone balls that turn up here and elsewhere, or the incredible newly discovered Burton Agnes and Folkton “drums”, little stone cylinders found buried with children in North Yorkshire, have no obvious practical purpose, and are decorated with designs that are obscure to our eyes, dense with irrecoverable significance. The same goes for the ancient “cup and ring” marks that exist on naturally occurring outcrops in the British landscape, in great quantity in places like Kilmartin Glen in Argyll. “I like the idea that each is a prayer or a mantra … I think the meaning may be in the making,” muses Wilkin of the patterns.

2003年的一天，在这座小房子的窗外，这位前房东在水槽里洗蔬菜时，看到一把犁在刀刃之间夹着一块砖石，犁发出嘎吱嘎吱的声音。它是新石器时代的石头。农民联系了当地的考古学家。爱德蒙说：“多年来，我们一直在开车经过这个地方，以为它是一座天然山脊。”他说，这里不是隐藏在普通视线中的古代遗迹。巨大的和解是一个持续的启示——然而，也许并不完全令人惊讶。这些岛屿上到处都是新石器时代的遗迹。爱德蒙告诉我，他曾经采访过奥卡迪亚家族，询问他们壁炉架上的古代石斧和其他发现物，其中有很多关于这位或那位曾祖父母发现的故事。一些新石器时代的发现，比如出现在这里和其他地方的复杂雕刻的石球，或者令人难以置信的新发现的伯顿·阿格尼斯和民间传说的“鼓”，即在北约克郡与孩子们一起发现的小石柱，没有明显的实际用途，并用我们看不清的设计装饰，具有不可恢复的意义。同样的道理也适用于古老的“杯子和戒指”标记，这些标记存在于英国自然形成的露头上，大量存在于阿盖尔的基尔马丁峡谷等地。“我喜欢每一个都是一个祈祷或咒语的想法……我认为其意义可能正在形成中，”这些模式的威尔金沉思道。

The buildings of the Ness of Brodgar are also decorated and inscribed, thickly but often subtly, with patterns – lozenges, cross-hatching, sometimes a kind of butterfly shape. At times the marks can be hard to make out. “One building had been exposed for several years and it wasn’t until the sun hit it in a particular way that we began to see it,” Card says. “We find more in August than July, in the raking light,” adds Mitchell.

布罗德加尼斯的建筑也有装饰和雕刻，厚重但往往微妙，有图案——菱形、交叉图案，有时是蝴蝶形状。有时这些痕迹很难辨认。卡德说：“一栋建筑已经暴露了好几年，直到太阳以某种特定的方式照射到它，我们才开始看到它。”。米切尔补充道：“在倾斜的光线下，我们在8月份发现的比7月份更多。”。

These patterns, these subtle strokes in the stone, have their echoes throughout the British Museum exhibition. Wilkin tells me he wanted to tell a story of influence and connection, of ideas travelling beyond the horizon, of people on the move. That starts with those who trudged across Doggerland when Britain’s east coast was still connected to continental Europe, and continues with those who brought farming to Britain from across the Channel about 6,000 years ago, to the “Beaker people” who arrived from around 2500BC, all but replacing them. The story is almost unimaginably long, told through artefacts from the haunting headdress fashioned by hunter-gatherers from deer antlers 11,000 years ago in Star Carr, Yorkshire, all the way to the outrageously strange conical hats fashioned from gold in France and Germany up to 8,000 years later. Not to mention the spellbinding teacup-size vessel, shaped from a single lump of amber about 1750BC, that’s a treasure of the Hove Museum and ought to be better known, and what I can’t help thinking of as “the amber spyglass” – a disk of translucent amber set in bronze, made in Denmark around 1200BC, that might once, possibly, have been use to “catch” the sun.

这些图案，石头上这些微妙的笔触，在大英博物馆的展览中都有回响。威尔金告诉我，他想讲一个关于影响和联系的故事，关于超越地平线的想法，关于流动的人的故事。这始于那些在英国东海岸仍与欧洲大陆相连时跋涉于多格兰的人，以及那些在大约6000年前将农业从海峡对岸带到英国的人，以及从公元前2500年左右来到英国的“烧杯人”，几乎全部取代了他们。这个故事几乎是难以想象的漫长，从11000年前约克郡斯塔卡尔（Star Carr，Yorkshire）狩猎采集者用鹿角制作的令人难以忘怀的头饰，到8000年后法国和德国用黄金制作的奇怪圆锥形帽子，都是通过人工制品讲述的。更不用说约公元前1750年由一块琥珀制成的迷人的茶杯大小的器皿了，这是霍夫博物馆的瑰宝，应该更为人所知，我忍不住想到的是“琥珀望远镜”——一个镶嵌在青铜中的半透明琥珀圆盘，约公元前1200年在丹麦制造，可能曾经，已经被用来“捕捉”太阳了。

Why the peoples of Orkney, and Wiltshire, and many other places in Britain, built vast monuments of stone 5,000 years ago, is a question that can never be definitively answered – and when we try, we almost certainly say more about ourselves than the real intentions of our distant ancestors. When I ask Edmonds what he thinks – we are standing in the little reconstructed neolithic house at Skara Brae at the time, sheltering from a hailstorm with a flask of coffee – he offers one word, albeit with a question mark: “Hubris?” Humans, he argues, seem to build remarkable monuments when they are either establishing power – or feeling under threat or pressure. Maybe these far distant people, their lives so alien and so unreachable, would find something to recognise in us, after all.

为什么奥克尼、威尔特郡和英国其他许多地方的人民在5000年前建造了巨大的石碑，这是一个永远无法确定答案的问题——当我们尝试时，几乎可以肯定的是，我们谈论的更多的是我们自己，而不是我们远祖的真正意图。当我问爱德蒙他是怎么想的——我们当时正站在斯卡拉布雷重建的新石器时代小房子里，喝着一瓶咖啡躲避冰雹——他给出了一个词，尽管带有问号：“傲慢？”他认为，当人类要么建立权力，要么感受到威胁或压力时，他们似乎在建造非凡的纪念碑。也许这些遥远的人，他们的生活如此陌生，如此遥不可及，终究会在我们身上发现一些东西。
