Is Sony's 140MB MiniDisc Drive the Next Betamax? (1996)
索尼的140MB MiniDisc驱动下一个槟子吗？ （1996）

2021-06-13 18:06:06

From the January 1996 Issue of PC World Sony has great hopes for its MiniDisc Data format as the next-generation mass storage media. And why not? On the surface, it has a lot going for it. A blank 2.5-inch magneto-optical MiniDisc offers 140MB of rewritable storage, and Sony promises the discs can be rewritten more than a million times with no loss of data integrity. The battery-powered MDH-10 drive's portability and its high-capacity MiniDiscs allow for easy transfer of data from a desktop to a notebook PC. And the Macintosh- and PC-compatible MiniDiscs can handle a hybrid of read-only and rewritable sections. The drive can also play back audio Mini-Discs (but Sony supports only the disc format). With a footprint about the size of an average Walkman, the MDH-10 drive is smaller than the Iomega Zip Drive and weighs in at just 12 ounces, including its rechargeable lithium-ion battery pack. The drive also works with the AC adapter that's included or with three AA alkaline batteries. But the MDH-10 and its MiniDiscs are expensive: The drive lists at $769.95, and each blank disc is $29.95. Compared to Iomega's $199 Zip Drive, which uses 100MB floppy-like disks that cost under $20 each, and Syquest's $240 portable EQ-135 drive, the MDH-10 is no bargain. This is especially true when you consider that the MDH-10 requires Sony's $259 SCSI PC Card (or a comparable PC Card) for your notebook and a SCSI port for your desktop. While the MDH-10 and MiniDisc have great storage potential, Sony is simply aced out by more affordable technologies like the Zip Drive's. - Michael S.
