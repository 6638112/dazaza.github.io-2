For more than a century, Popular Mechanics has provided life-saving advice for outlasting storms, surviving outdoors, and preparing for disaster. Find out how to survive anything right here.

You have a late night and an early flight. Not long after takeoff, you drift to sleep. Suddenly, you're wide awake. There's cold air rushing everywhere, and sound. Intense, horrible sound. Where am I?, you think. Where's the plane?

您有一个深夜，一个早起的航班。起飞后不久，您便开始入睡。突然，你清醒了。那里到处都是冷空气，声音很大。强烈的声音。我在哪里？飞机在哪里？

Things are bad. But now's the time to focus on the good news. (Yes, it goes beyond surviving the destruction of your aircraft.) Although gravity is against you, another force is working in your favor: time. Believe it or not, you're better off up here than if you'd slipped from the balcony of your high-rise hotel room after one too many drinks last night.

事情不好。但是现在是时候关注好消息了。 （是的，它不仅仅能够摧毁飞机。）尽管重力对着您，但另一种力量正在对您有利：时间。信不信由你，比起昨晚喝了太多酒后从高层酒店房间的阳台溜走，你在这里过得更好。

Or at least you will be. Oxygen is scarce at these heights. By now, hypoxia is starting to set in. You'll be unconscious soon, and you'll cannonball at least a mile before waking up again. When that happens, remember what you are about to read. The ground, after all, is your next destination.

至少你会的。在这些高度，氧气稀缺。到现在，缺氧开始出现。您很快就会失去知觉，并且在至少醒来一英里之前会炮弹。发生这种情况时，请记住要阅读的内容。毕竟，地面是您的下一个目的地。

Granted, the odds of surviving a 6-mile plummet are extra­ordinarily slim, but at this point you've got nothing to lose by understanding your situation. There are two ways to fall out of a plane. The first is to free-fall, or drop from the sky with absolutely no protection or means of slowing your descent. The second is to become a wreckage rider, a term coined by Massachusetts-based amateur historian Jim Hamilton, who developed the Free Fall Research Page—an online database of nearly every imaginable human plummet.

当然，在6英里的直线下降中幸存的几率非常小，但是在这一点上，了解您的情况并不会给您带来任何损失。有两种方法可以脱离飞机。首先是在没有任何保护措施或放慢下降速度的情况下自由下落或从天上掉下来。第二个是成为残骸骑手，这个术语是由马萨诸塞州的业余历史学家吉姆·汉密尔顿（Jim Hamilton）创造的，他开发了“自由落体研究页面”（Free Fall Research Page），该数据库是几乎所有可以想象的人类坠落的在线数据库。

That classification means you have the advantage of being attached to a chunk of the plane. In 1972, Serbian flight attendant Vesna Vulovic was traveling in a DC-9 over Czechoslovakia when it blew up. She fell 33,000 feet, wedged between her seat, a catering trolley, a section of aircraft, and the body of another crew member, landing on—then sliding down—a snowy incline before coming to a stop, severely injured but alive.

该分类意味着您具有被附加到平面的一部分的优势。 1972年，塞尔维亚空姐维斯纳·武洛维奇（Vesna Vulovic）爆炸时正乘坐DC-9飞机飞越捷克斯洛伐克。她跌落了33,000英尺，被楔在座位，一架餐饮手推车，一架飞机和另一名机组人员的身体之间，在停下来之前着陆，然后滑下了雪坡，重伤但还活着。

Surviving a plunge surrounded by a semi-protective cocoon of debris is more common than surviving a pure free-fall, including perennial Ripley's Believe It or Not superstar Alan Magee—blown from his B-17 on a 1943 mission over France. The New Jersey airman fell 20,000 feet and crashed into a train station; he was subsequently captured by German troops, who were astonished at his survival.

比起纯粹的自由落体，幸存于被半保护性碎片茧包围的暴跌中更常见，包括常年的里普利的信不信由你巨星艾伦·马吉（Alan Magee）-他在1943年对法国的一次任务中从B-17炸飞。这名新泽西州的飞行员跌落了20,000英尺，坠毁在火车站。随后，他被德军俘虏，他的生存感到惊讶。

Whether you're attached to crumpled fuselage or just plain falling, the concept you'll be most interested in is terminal velocity. As gravity pulls you toward earth, you go faster. But like any moving object, you create drag—more as your speed increases. When downward force equals upward resistance, acceleration stops. You max out.

不管您是皱巴巴的机身还是只是摔倒，您最感兴趣的概念是航速。随着重力将您拉向地球，您走得更快。但是，就像任何移动的物体一样，您会创建阻力-随着速度的增加而增加。当向下的力等于向上的阻力时，加速停止。你最大了。

Depending on your size and weight, and factors such as air density, your speed at that moment will be about 120 mph—and you'll get there after a surprisingly brief bit of falling: just 1,500 feet, about the same height as Chicago's Willis Tower. Equal speed means you hit the ground with equal force. The difference is the clock. Body meets Windy City sidewalk in 12 seconds.

根据您的身材和体重，以及诸如空气密度等因素，您当时的速度将约为120英里/小时，经过短暂的意外坠落，您将到达那里：只有1,500英尺，大约与芝加哥威利斯大厦。相等的速度意味着您以相等的力击中地面。区别在于时钟。身体在12秒内遇到Windy City人行道。

From an airplane's cruising altitude, you'll have almost enough time to read this entire article.

从飞机的巡航高度，您将有足够的时间阅读整篇文章。

By now, you've descended into breathable air. You sputter into consciousness. At this altitude, you've got roughly 2 minutes until impact. Your plan is simple. You will enter a Zen state and decide to live. You will understand, as Hamilton notes, "that it isn't the fall that kills you—it's the landing."

到目前为止，您已经进入了透气的状态。你溅入意识中。在此高度下，您大约需要2分钟才能撞击。您的计划很简单。您将进入禅宗状态并决定生活。正如汉密尔顿所指出的那样，您会明白，不是坠落致您死亡，而是落地。

But at what? Magee's landing on the stone floor of that French train station was softened by the skylight he crashed through a moment earlier. Glass hurts, but it gives. So does grass. Haystacks and bushes have cushioned surprised-to-be-alive free-fallers. Trees aren't bad, though they tend to skewer. Snow? Absolutely. Swamps? With their mucky, plant-covered surface, even more awesome.

但是在什么？马吉（Magee）降落在那个法国火车站的石地板上，由于他在刚才撞车的天窗而变得柔和。玻璃很痛，但它给人。草也是如此。干草堆和灌木丛缓和了令人惊奇的活着的自由下落。树木不错，尽管它们容易串起来。雪？绝对地。沼泽？泥泞，植物覆盖的表面更加令人赞叹。

Hamilton documents one case of a sky diver who, upon total parachute failure, was saved by bouncing off high-tension wires. Contrary to popular belief, water is an awful choice. Like concrete, liquid doesn't compress. Hitting the ocean is essentially the same as colliding with a sidewalk, Hamilton explains, except that pavement (perhaps unfortunately) won't "open up and swallow your shattered body."

汉密尔顿（Hamilton）记录了一个跳伞者的案例，该跳伞者在完全降落伞失灵时通过弹跳高压电线而得救。与普遍的看法相反，水是一个糟糕的选择。像混凝土一样，液体不会压缩。汉密尔顿解释说，击打海洋与撞上人行道本质上是一样的，除了人行道（也许不幸）不会张开并吞下破碎的身体。

With a target in mind, the next consideration is body position. To slow your descent, emulate a sky diver. Spread your arms and legs, present your chest to the ground, and arch your back and head upward. This adds friction and helps you maneuver. But don't relax. This is not your landing pose.

考虑到目标，接下来要考虑的是身体姿势。要减慢下降速度，请模仿跳伞运动员。展开您的手臂和腿，将您的胸部放在地面上，然后将背部和头部拱起。这会增加摩擦并帮助您进行机动。但是不要放松。这不是你的着陆姿势。

The question of how to achieve ground contact remains, regrettably, given your predicament, a subject of debate. A 1942 study in the journal War Medicine noted "distribution and compensation of pressure play large parts in the defeat of injury." Recommendation: wide-body impact. But a 1963 report by the Federal Aviation Agency argued that shifting into the classic sky diver's landing stance—feet together, heels up, flexed knees and hips—best increases survivability. The same study noted that training in wrestling and acrobatics would help people survive falls. Martial arts were deemed especially useful for hard-surface impacts: "A 'black belt' expert can reportedly crack solid wood with a single blow," the authors wrote, speculating that such skills might be transferable.

令人遗憾的是，鉴于您的困境，仍然是辩论的话题，如何实现地面接触仍然是一个问题。 1942年在《战争医学》杂志上进行的一项研究指出，压力的分配和补偿在挫败伤害中起着很大的作用。建议：宽体冲击。但是，美国联邦航空局（Federal Aviation Agency）在1963年的一份报告中指出，改用经典的跳伞运动员的着陆姿态（双脚并拢，脚跟抬起，膝盖和臀部屈曲）最好地提高了生存能力。该研究还指出，进行摔跤和杂技训练将帮助人们在摔跤中幸存。武术被认为对硬表面撞击特别有用：＆＃39; A黑带＆＃39;据报道，专家可以一口气将实木开裂，＆＃34;作者写道，推测这些技能可能是可转让的。

The ultimate learn-by-doing experience might be a lesson from Japanese parachutist Yasuhiro Kubo, who holds the world record in the activity's banzai category. The sky diver tosses his chute from the plane and then jumps out after it, waiting as long as possible to retrieve it, put it on and pull the ripcord. In 2000, Kubo—starting from 9,842 feet—fell for 50 seconds before recovering his gear. A safer way to practice your technique would be at one of the wind-tunnel simulators found at about a dozen U.S. theme parks and malls.

最终的边做边学经验可能是日本跳伞者久保康宏的一课，他在这项运动的万岁类别中保持世界纪录。跳伞者将他的溜槽从飞机上扔下，然后跳下跳伞，等待尽可能长的时间将其捡起，戴上并拉扯绳索。 2000年，久保-从9,842英尺高处开始-下落了50秒钟，然后恢复了自己的装备。在大约12个美国主题公园和购物中心中发现的一种风洞模拟器中，是一种更安全地练习技术的方法。

But neither will help with the toughest part: sticking the landing. For that you might consider—though it's not exactly advisable—a leap off the world's highest bridge, France's Millau Viaduct; its platform towers 891 feet over mostly spongy farmland.

但是，任何一项都不能帮助最艰难的部分：坚持着陆。为此，您可能会考虑-尽管不是完全明智的做法-跳下了世界最高的桥梁，法国的Millau高架桥；它的平台高出大部分海绵状农田891英尺。

Water landings—if you must—require quick decision-making. Studies of bridge-jump survivors indicate that a feet-first, knife-like entry (aka "the pencil") best optimizes your odds of resurfacing. The famed cliff divers of Acapulco, however, tend to assume a head-down position, with the fingers of each hand locked together, arms outstretched, protecting the head. Whichever you choose, first assume the free-fall position for as long as you can. Then, if a feet-first entry is inevitable, the most important piece of advice, for reasons both unmentionable and easily understood, is to clench your butt.

水上降落（如果需要）需要快速决策。跳桥幸存者的研究表明，像脚刀一样先入刀（又名“铅笔”）可以最大程度地优化您重铺路面的几率。但是，阿卡普尔科（Acapulco）著名的悬崖跳水运动员倾向于低头，每只手的手指锁定在一起，双臂伸出，保护头部。无论您选择哪种方式，都首先要尽可能长时间地保持自由落体位置。然后，如果不可避免要先踩脚，出于种种不易理解且易于理解的原因，最重要的建议就是紧握臀部。

No matter the surface, definitely don't land on your head. In a 1977 "Study of Impact Tolerance Through Free-Fall Investigations," researchers at the Highway Safety Research Institute found that the major cause of death in falls—they examined drops from buildings, bridges and the occasional elevator shaft (oops!)—was cranial contact. If you have to arrive top-down, sacrifice your good looks and land on your face, rather than the back or top of your head. You might also consider flying with a pair of goggles in your pocket, Hamilton says, since you're likely to get watery eyes—impairing accuracy—on the way down.

无论表面如何，绝对不要落在您的头上。在1977年通过自由落体调查进行的耐冲击性研究中，公路安全研究所的研究人员发现，跌倒的主要死因是颅骨接触，他们检查了建筑物，桥梁和偶尔出现的电梯竖井（哎呀！）掉落的物体。如果必须自上而下到达，则要牺牲自己的好看并落在脸上，而不要牺牲头部或头部的后部。汉密尔顿说，您可能还考虑在口袋里放一副护目镜，因为在下降的过程中您可能会流眼泪，从而影响准确性。

Statistically speaking, it's best to be a flight crew member, a child, or traveling in a military aircraft. Over the past four decades, there have been at least a dozen commercial airline crashes with just one survivor. Of those documented, four of the survivors were crew, like the flight attendant Vulovic, and seven were passengers under the age of 18. That includes Mohammed el-Fateh Osman, a 2-year-old wreckage rider who lived through the crash of a Boeing jet in Sudan in 2003.

从统计上讲，最好是机组人员，小孩或乘坐军机旅行。在过去的四十年中，至少有十二起商业航班坠毁，其中只有一名幸存者。在记录在案的人员中，有4名幸存者是乘务员，例如空姐Vulovic，还有7名是18岁以下的乘客。其中包括2岁的残骸骑手Mohammed el-Fateh Osman，他在一次飞机坠机事故中幸存下来。波音公司在苏丹的喷气式飞机，2003年。

Crew survival may be related to better restraint systems, but there's no consensus on why children seem to pull through falls more often. The Federal Aviation Agency study notes that kids, especially those under the age of 4, have more flexible skeletons, more relaxed muscle tonus, and a higher proportion of subcutaneous fat, which helps protect internal organs. Smaller people—whose heads are lower than the seat backs in front of them—are better shielded from debris in a plane that's coming apart. Lower body weight reduces terminal velocity, plus reduced surface area decreases the chance of impalement upon landing.

乘员生存可能与更好的约束系统有关，但是对于为什么儿童似乎更常经历跌倒尚无共识。联邦航空局（Federal Aviation Agency）的研究指出，尤其是4岁以下的孩子，骨骼更加灵活，肌肉突触更加放松，皮下脂肪比例更高，有助于保护内部器官。较小的人（其头比其前面的靠背低），在飞机散落的平面中，可以更好地防止碎屑进入。较低的体重会降低最终速度，加上减小的表面积会减少着陆时刺穿的机会。

The ground. Like a Shaolin master, you are at peace and prepared. Impact. You're alive. What next? If you're lucky, you might find that your injuries are minor, stand up and smoke a celebratory cigarette, as British tail gunner Nicholas Alkemade did in 1944 after landing in snowy bushes following an 18,000-foot plummet. More likely, you'll have tough work ahead.

地面。像少林大师一样，您处于安宁与准备状态。影响。你还活着。接下来是什么？如果幸运的话，您可能会发现自己受了轻伤，站起来抽着庆祝的香烟，就像英国尾炮手尼古拉斯·阿尔克玛德（Nicholas Alkemade）在18000英尺的直线下降后降落在白雪皑皑的灌木丛中后于1944年所做的那样。您更有可能面临艰巨的工作。

Follow the example of Juliane Koepcke. On Christmas Eve 1971, the Lockheed Electra she was traveling in exploded over the Amazon. The next morning, the 17-year-old German awoke on the jungle floor, strapped into her seat, surrounded by fallen holiday gifts. Injured and alone, she pushed the death of her mother, who'd been seated next to her on the plane, out of her mind. Instead, she remembered advice from her father, a biologist: To find civilization when lost in the jungle, follow water. Koepcke waded from tiny streams to larger ones. She passed crocodiles and poked the mud in front of her with a stick to scare away stingrays. She had lost one shoe in the fall and was wearing a ripped miniskirt. Her only food was a bag of candy, and she had nothing but dark, dirty water to drink. She ignored her broken collarbone and her wounds, infested with maggots.

跟随朱利安·科普克（Juliane Koepcke）的例子。 1971年圣诞节前夕，她乘坐的洛克希德·伊莱克特拉（Lockheed Electra）在亚马逊河上爆炸。第二天早上，这位17岁的德国人在丛林地板上醒来，被绑在座位上，周围是节日礼物。她独自一人受伤，使她的母亲无法自拔，母亲在飞机上坐在她旁边坐了下来。相反，她想起了父亲父亲的生物学家的建议：要在丛林中迷失时寻找文明，请跟随水流。 Koepcke从微小的溪流到更大的溪流。她通过了鳄鱼，并用一根棍子戳了戳自己面前的泥巴，以吓退黄貂鱼。她在秋天丢了一只鞋，穿着一条破破的超短裙。她唯一的食物是一袋糖果，除了黑暗，肮脏的水外，她别无选择。她忽略了断了的锁骨和伤口，身上满了got。

On the tenth day, she rested on the bank of the Shebonya River. When she stood up again, she saw a canoe tethered to the shoreline. It took her hours to climb the embankment to a hut, where, the next day, a group of lumberjacks found her. The incident was seen as a miracle in Peru, and free-fall statistics seem to support those arguing for divine intervention: According to the Geneva-based Aircraft Crashes Record Office, 118,934 people have died in 15,463 plane crashes between 1940 and 2008.

第十天，她在Shebonya河岸上休息。当她再次站起来时，她看到一条独木舟系在海岸线上。她花了几个小时才把路堤爬到小屋里，第二天，一群伐木工人找到了她。该事件被视为秘鲁的一个奇迹，自由落体的统计数字似乎支持那些主张神的干预的人：根据位于日内瓦的飞机失事记录办公室的说法，在1940年至2008年之间，有15934起飞机失事中有118934人死亡。

Even when you add failed-chute sky divers, Hamilton's tally of confirmed or plausible lived-to-tell-about-it incidents is only 157, with 42 occurring at heights over 10,000 feet.

But Koepcke never saw survival as a matter of fate. She can still recall the first moments of her fall from the plane, as she spun through the air in her seat. That wasn't under her control, but what happened when she regained consciousness was. "I had been able to make the correct decision—to leave the scene of the crash," she says now. And because of experience at her parents' biological research station, she says, "I did not feel fear. I knew how to move in the forest and the river, in which I had to swim with dangerous animals like caimans and piranhas."
