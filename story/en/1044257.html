Nintendo has a copyright on the video games, their visual appearance, sound, storyline, etc. As such, any recording of the gameplay of their video games are going to be a derivative work, based on the video game, and thus potentially infringing on the copyright of the originating work. As a result, Nintendo, and really any video game developer/publisher, can claim copyright infringement on any video showing gameplay from one of their games. In reality, most developers/publishers don't take down gameplay videos, and often actively encourage it, because they don't view the videos as a replacement for the actual game, and often see such videos increase sales as viewers opt to play the game for themselves. In this instance, Nintendo is using the fact that the hacking videos that show some gameplay of the built-in games are technically copyright infringement to file DMCA requests to get the videos take down. When infringement complaints are issued, a possible defense is to declare fair use, but that largely only applies for when using the copyrighted work is essential to the later work. So, for example, a game review can argue it's fair to show footage of the game to comment about the game's quality. Since it's not essential to show the built-in games in order to show how to hack the handheld, that might not be a compelling argument in this instance.
