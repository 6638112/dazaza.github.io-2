Exploring the role of competitive brain processes in artistic cognition
探索竞争性大脑过程在艺术认知中的作用

2021-01-20 12:16:02

For many years, neuroscientists worldwide have been trying to understand the neural and cognitive processes underpinning artistic expression. While past findings have identified a number of brain areas that could be associated with the creation of different forms of art, scientists have yet to attain a full and comprehensive picture of the neural processes behind artistic cognition. Researchers at the Lyon Neuroscience Research Center (CRNL) have recently carried out a study aimed at putting the known pieces of this puzzle together by reviewing past studies investigating the neural processes behind artistic cognition. Their paper, published by MIT Press in the journal Leonardo, provides valuable new insight about the role that competitive brain processes could play in artistic cognition.

多年以来，世界各地的神经科学家一直试图了解支撑艺术表达的神经和认知过程。尽管过去的发现已经确定了许多与不同形式的艺术创作有关的大脑区域，但科学家们尚未完全了解艺术认知背后的神经过程。里昂神经科学研究中心（CRNL）的研究人员最近进行了一项研究，旨在通过回顾研究艺术认知背后的神经过程的过去研究，将这个难题的已知部分放在一起。他们的论文由麻省理工学院出版社在列昂纳多（Leonardo）杂志上发表，提供了有价值的新见解，说明了竞争性大脑过程在艺术认知中可能发挥的作用。

Over the past two decades, the human understanding of the brain has changed significantly, and neuroscientists have started viewing it as an ecosystem. This ecosystem is composed of processes that either cooperate or compete with each other.

在过去的二十年中，人类对大脑的理解发生了巨大变化，神经科学家开始将其视为生态系统。这个生态系统由相互协作或竞争的过程组成。

"Competition between brain processes leads to fascinating dynamic interactions that can explain many performances, such as learning, memory and many real-life observations," Dezso Nemeth, one of the researchers who carried out the study, told Medical Xpress. "In line with this new outlook on the brain, we can better understand how we are able to perceive, see and represent the world around us. One of the key functions discussed in our paper is implicit learning, which is closely related to intuition."

In their paper, Nemeth and his colleagues took a first step toward understanding artistic cognition from a cognitive neuroscience perspective. Their main objective was to understand how the human brain deals with art and how an artist's brain perceives the world, builds representations and uses neural processes related to intuition. In psychology, the term intuition refers to the human ability to understand or examine something instinctively, without consciously reasoning about it.

Nemeth和他的同事在他们的论文中迈出了从认知神经科学的角度理解艺术认知的第一步。他们的主要目的是了解人脑如何与艺术打交道，以及艺术家的大脑如何感知世界，建立表征并使用与直觉相关的神经过程。在心理学中，直觉一词是指人类无意识地对本能进行理解或检查的能力。

"Our paper is a theoretical instigation in which we connected the results of past empirical studies in cognitive neuroscience, particularly those focusing on artistic cognition," Nemeth explained. "Ultimately, we sought to explain the role of implicit learning processes in artistic cognition, or how the competition between different brain networks can lead to a more effective artistic intuition."

After reviewing past papers related to artistic cognition, Nemeth and his colleagues introduced the idea that when it comes to producing art, "less is more." More specifically, they suggested that "weaker" prefrontal circuits, which are related to (i.e., that allow humans to control their behavior and focus on a task at hand, such as working memory and flexible thinking), can actually lead to more effective artistic cognition. The researchers refer to this phenomenon as the Andras effect.

"For example, if a photographer can tune down her and access to long-term memories, she can perceive a 'different world'; a world without expectations or past memories," Nemeth said. "We can call this intuitive photography."

In their paper, Nemeth and his colleagues introduce a new way of thinking about artistic cognition from a neuroscientific standpoint. While the ideas they presented are fascinating, so far, they are merely theoretical and based on past observations. In the future, the researchers would like to conduct investigations aimed at confirming their hypotheses and gathering empirical evidence for what they refer to as the Andras effect.

Nemeth和他的同事在他们的论文中从神经科学的角度介绍了一种关于艺术认知的新方法。尽管他们提出的想法引人入胜，但到目前为止，它们只是理论上的，并基于过去的观察。将来，研究人员希望进行调查，以证实他们的假设并收集关于他们所谓的安德拉斯效应的经验证据。

"With my research team, we want to pin down the neural and cognitive bases of the competitive learning processes in the brain," Nemeth added. "We use several methods such as noninvasive brain stimulation and the measuring of functional connectivity. Another aim of our future work will be to initiate greater communication between scientists and artists, in order to understand artistic intuition and cognition as a whole.
