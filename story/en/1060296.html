At Home Among the Birds: An Interview with Jonathan Meiburg
在鸟类中的家中：采访Jonathan Meiburg

2021-04-26 15:04:07

Jonathan Meiburg was born in Baltimore, Maryland, in 1976 and grew up in the southeastern United States. In 1997, he received a Thomas J. Watson Fellowship to travel to remote communities around the world, a year-long journey that sparked an enduring fascination with islands, birds, and the deep history of the living world. Meiburg explores these passions in his new book, A Most Remarkable Creature , which traces the evolution of the wildlife and landscapes of South America through the lives of the unusual falcons called caracaras. Like the omnivorous birds at the heart of his book, Meiburg is more generalist than specialist. He’s written reviews, features, and interviews for publications including The Believer , Talkhouse , and The Appendix , on subjects ranging from the music of Brian Eno to a hidden exhibition hall at the American Museum of Natural History. He also conducted one of the last interviews with Peter Matthiessen. But he’s best known as a musician—albums and performances by his bands Loma and Shearwater have earned critical acclaim for many years, often winning praise from NPR, the New York Times , the Guardian , and Pitchfork . In 2018, Meiburg organized and performed in a three-night live reconstruction of David Bowie’s Berlin Trilogy for WNYC’s New Sounds program. He lives in central Texas. This interview was conducted electronically between there and Wilmington, North Carolina.

Jonathan Meiburg于1976年出生于马里兰州巴尔的摩，并在美国东南部长大。 1997年，他收到了一个托马斯J. Watson奖学金，前往全球的远程社区，这是一年长的旅程，激发了与岛屿，鸟类和生活世界的深刻历史的持久迷恋。 Meiburg在他的新书中探讨了这本最重要的生物，这是一种追踪南美洲野生动物的演变，通过称为Caracaras的异常猎鹰的生活。就像他书中心的杂食鸟一样，Meiburg比专家更广泛。他为包括信徒，谈判，展览和附录的出版物的书面评论，特征和访谈，在美国自然历史博物馆的一个隐藏的展厅到一个隐藏的展览馆。他还开展了彼得马特森的最后一次访谈。但他最知名为他的乐队和坟墓的音乐专辑和表演，多年来赢得了批评的好评，往往赢得了NPR，纽约时报，监护人和干草叉的赞誉。 2018年，Meiburg在David Bowie's Berlin Trilogy为WNYC的新声音计划中组织并进行了一次性重建。他住在德克萨斯州中部。这次采访是电子方式在北卡罗来纳州和威尔明顿之间进行的。

Recently an anhinga started roosting by the tidal creek that runs through my backyard. It’s fun to watch—the long snaky neck and the way it hangs its wings out to dry like laundry. Once, when the creek was clear, I watched it hunt, and it flew along underwater like a fish. I’ve been told that anhingas aren’t really supposed to be here, or that this is north of their range, which is shifting due to climate change.

最近，一个潮汐溪流通过我的后院跑的潮汐溪流栖息。看着漫长的鼻子和它悬挂它的方式很有趣，它可以像洗衣一样干燥。曾经，当小溪很清楚时，我看着它狩猎，它沿着水下飞来。我被告知，阿欣斯并不真正应该在这里，或者这是北方的范围，这是由于气候变化而转化。

To me anhingas are among the most beautiful birds, and maybe the most like their dinosaurian ancestors of any birds. A lot of people think they’re sort of goofy, and I can see that, too—the tiny heads, the absurdly long necks and flight feathers, the overall sense that they’re a prototype someone meant to refine later. They have wonderful nicknames like “snakebird” and “water turkey.” I love how you can see them soaring way up in the sky for no apparent reason, like ravens with long, skinny necks, even though they make their living wading around on the bottoms of rivers.

对我来说，美味是最美丽的鸟类之一，也许是最喜欢任何鸟类的恐龙祖先。很多人认为他们是一种愚蠢的，我可以看到，太小的头部，荒谬的长颈和飞行羽毛，整体意义上是他们的原型意味着稍后改进。他们有精彩的绰号，如“蛇鸟”和“水土耳其”。我喜欢如何看到他们在天空中翱翔，因为没有明显的理由，就像长长，瘦脖子的乌鸦一样，即使他们在河口底部跋涉挥舞。

They need to stay underwater for a long time, so they have very little oil on their feathers. This helps them sink and stay down. But it means they have to spend a lot of time standing on perches and holding their wings out to dry, which is usually how you see them, and it locks them out of places that get really cold. I was used to seeing them in the southeastern U.S., especially along that elevated stretch of I-10 that crosses the Atchafalaya Basin—but they were thick along the river in southern Guyana when I visited, and they seemed completely at home among colorful, feathered weirdos like parrots, toucans, and capuchin birds.

他们需要长时间留下水下，所以他们的羽毛上有很少的油。这有助于他们下沉并留下来。但这意味着他们必须花费大量的时间站在栖息处，并将他们的翅膀拿出来干燥，这通常是你看到它们的方式，它将它们锁定在真正冷的地方。我被习惯在美国东南部看到他们，特别是沿着阿彻瓜山盆地的升高的I-10延伸 - 但是当我访问的时候，他们沿着杜兰纳南部的河流厚实，他们似乎完全在家里，羽毛奇怪的河流，巨嘴鲸和斗篷鸟类。

What role does this movement of animals between South and North America play in your story? I think of it all as one big blob of land with a canal in the middle.

南北和北美之间的动物在你的故事中发挥了什么作用？我认为这一切都是一个大斑的土地，中间的运河。

This question is pretty much at the heart of my book, which starts with Darwin scratching his head about why the striated caracara, a bold, social, curious bird of prey he met at the southern tip of South America, lived there and nowhere else. Trying to figure that out changed the way I thought about the world.

这个问题几乎在我的书的核心上，从达尔文抓住他的脑袋，他在南美洲南部的南端遇到了德国的Caracara，大胆，社会，好奇的猎物，居住在那里，无处可去。试图弄明白，改变了我对世界的看法。

Expand on that, for those of us who don’t instantly make the connection between “strange bird confined to the southernmost part of South America” and “the world.”

扩展到这一点，对于我们不立即建立“陌生鸟局限于南美洲最南端”和“世界”之间的联系的人。

Until I started trying to understand where the caracaras came from, I’d never realized that North and South America, biologically and geologically, are basically strangers to each other. Lumping them together as a single “New World,” as Europeans did, really doesn’t make much sense. Tectonic forces kept the Americas apart for a hundred million years.  When they finally joined hands at the isthmus of Panama a few million years ago—a short time, if you’re a paleontologist—two sets of animals who’d pursued completely separate evolutionary journeys confronted each other, which one scientist called “one of the most extraordinary events in the whole history of life.”

直到我开始试图了解康拉卡拉斯来自哪里，我从来没有意识到北美和南美，生物学和地质上，基本上是彼此的陌生人。随着欧洲人所做的，作为一个单一的“新世界”，他们一起把它们作为一个“新世界”。构造势力让美洲分开一亿年。当他们终于多百万年前在巴拿马的斯廷斯加入手时 - 很短的时间 - 如果你是一个古生物学家 - 两套动物追求完全独立的进化旅程，互相面对，一个科学家称之为“之一整个生命历史上最具非凡的事件。“

Caracaras are part of this story. Like the parrots, their ancestors probably entered South America through the forests of a warm Antarctica, and in the southern New World they mostly play the same roles crows play here—clever, social scavengers, like raccoons with wings. But unlike large crows, which have never found a foothold in South America, caracaras made it to the northern hemisphere thousands of years ago. We’ve found their fossil bones in California and Arkansas, and although they retreated south after people arrived in the Americas, they’re slowly coming north again. The website eBird says they’ve been seen recently in the Outer Banks.

Caracaras是这个故事的一部分。像鹦鹉一样，他们的祖先可能通过南极洲的森林进入南美洲，并且在南部的新世界中，他们大多扮演同样的角色乌鸦在这里玩的奇妙，社会清道夫，像狼人一样。但与大乌鸦不同，在南美洲从未在南美洲立足之上，Caracaras将它成为北半年多年前的北半球。我们在加利福尼亚州和阿肯色州找到了他们的化石骨头，虽然在人们到达美洲后，他们撤退了南方，他们慢慢地慢慢地走了。这个网站难怪说他们最近在外部银行看到了。

What hooked me was their personality. Beside the fact that they’re big, flashy birds of prey that behave more like crows, striated caracaras just don’t act like wild animals are “supposed” to. They swagger up like they’ve got as much right to be here as you do, and look you right in the eye. If you sit still they’ll start trying to take things out of your bag. I have a little video of one trying to figure out what to do with a pair of hiking poles. They’re utterly and confidently themselves, and they made me realize that the reactions of every wild animal I’d ever seen—hiding or fleeing in terror—weren’t how it always was. Animals had to learn to do that, from bitter experience.

有什么迷恋我是他们的个性。除了他们大的事实旁边，表现得更像乌鸦的猛禽，横向的Caracaras只是不要像野生动物一样表现为“假设”。他们像他们一样笑起来，就像在你一样，看着你的眼睛。如果你坐下，他们就会开始试图把东西从你的包里拿出来。我有一个小的视频，试图弄清楚与一对徒步的杆子有关。他们完全和自信地自信，他们让我意识到每个野生动物的反应都没有看到或逃离恐怖 - 并没有如何始终。从苦涩的经历中，动物必须学会这样做。

I should make clear that by “caracaras” I mean all ten species, a unique lineage of odd falcons that lives mostly in South America. Striated caracaras are the rarest, but they’re all equally wonderful in their own ways. One tropical species, for example, lives in large family groups that raise one chick at a time, and survives on a diet of wasps’ nests and fruit. They all appear in the book. There’s no such thing as “the caracara” any more than there’s a bird called “the eagle.”

我应该明确说“Caracaras”，我的意思是所有十种种类，一个独特的奇怪猎鹰队，主要在南美洲生活。条纹的康拉加拉斯是最稀有的，但它们的方式都是非常精彩的。例如，一种热带物种，例如，生活在大型家庭群体中，一次举起一只小鸡，并在黄蜂巢和水果的饮食上存活。它们都出现在书中。没有像“卡拉卡拉”那样的东西，而不是一个叫做“鹰”的鸟。

You were approaching these birds as a naturalist. When did they turn into a book?

你正在接近这些鸟类是一个自然主义者。他们什么时候变成一本书？

A long time ago, you told me, “Never write a book if you can help it.” I couldn’t help it. Before I met striated caracaras, I’d vaguely thought scientists knew most of what there was to know about animals, especially animals surrounded by celebrity wildlife like penguins and whales, so I was naively surprised that I’d never heard of these unusual birds, then genuinely surprised at how little was known about them—even though they’d also transfixed Darwin, who wondered the same things. What were they? Why did they act like this? Why did they live at the bottom of the world and nowhere else?

很久以前，你告诉我，“如果你可以帮助它，从不写一本书。”我无法帮助它。在我遇到的康帕拉斯之前，我含糊地认为科学家大部分都知道关于动物的大部分知识，特别是名人野生动物像企鹅和鲸鱼所包围的动物，所以我真的很惊讶地惊讶于我从来没有听说过这些不寻常的鸟类，然后真正惊讶于他们对他们所知的几点 - 尽管他们也经历了同样的事情，但是他们也会被修复达尔文。他们是什么？为什么他们这样的行为？他们为什么住在世界的底部，无所事事？

You’ve mentioned Darwin, but surely some other people or peoples noticed them, even so far south.

你已经提到了达尔文，但肯定是其他一些人或人民注意到他们，即使是南方的。

Oh, yeah. The Inca emperors were allowed to wear the feathers of Andean caracaras, and dancers dressed as these birds still feature in solstice parades in highland Ecuador and Peru. A crested caracara showed the Aztecs’ ancestors where to build Tenochtitlán—now Mexico City—and Toba people in northern Argentina told an ethnographer from the United States that the supernatural hero Carancho—another name for a crested caracara—entrusted humans with the secrets of fire and medicine.

哦耶。印加皇帝被允许佩戴Andean Caracaras的羽毛，并且随着这些鸟类的舞蹈演员仍然存在于Highland厄瓜多尔和秘鲁的Solstice Parades。一个有冠的Caracara展示了Aztecs的祖先，在阿根廷北部的墨西哥城和托巴人中展示了托茶，从美国讲述了一个什洛拉伯人，即超自然的英雄Carancho - 另一个名字为一个有冠的Caracara-委托人委托的人的秘密和医学。

When you compare that with the way Europeans regarded striated caracaras in the Falklands, it’s hard not to shake your head. Sealers and whalers called them “flying devils,” and the enterprising colonists who turned the islands into a sheep farm the size of Connecticut slapped a bounty on the birds’ beaks, nearly driving them to extinction. A marooned sealer declared them “the most mischievous of all the feathered creation,” and even Darwin called them “false eagles” who “ill become so high a rank.” They’re protected now, and minds are changing—though as I say in the book, I’m not sure how much time they have left if we don’t take some fairly wild measures to save them. There are about the same number of adult striated caracaras as there are giant pandas in the wild.

当你与欧洲人在福克兰队的欧洲人被认为是康复的康拉卡拉拉斯的方式相比，很难撼动你的头。封口机和捕鲸者称为他们“飞行魔鬼”，并将岛屿转变为绵羊场的进取的殖民者，康涅狄格的大小拍摄了山的喙，几乎驱使他们灭绝。一个Masoy的封口人宣称他们“最淘汰所有羽毛的创作，甚至达尔文叫他们”假老鹰“谁”生病变得如此高的排名。“他们现在受到保护，思想正在变化 - 就像我在书中所说的那样，如果我们没有采取一些相当狂野的措施来拯救他们，我就不确定他们离开了多少时间。大约有相同数量的成人条纹的康拉卡拉拉斯，因为野外有巨大的熊猫。

Is it true that we were once members of a nameless band that did two benefit shows then instantly broke up?

我们曾经是一个无名乐队的成员是真的，那么瞬间会分手吗？

Is it also true that you are now in a pretty famous band called Shearwater that just put out a record produced by Brian Eno?

你现在还在一个叫做牧羊犬的漂亮着名乐队中也是如此，刚刚推出了Brian Eno制作的记录？

Not quite. Shearwater has done seven albums for Matador and Sub Pop since 2006, and I’m working on an eighth right now. As for Eno, he mixed the final track, “ Homing,” on the most recent album by Loma, a band I started a couple of years ago with Emily Cross as the singer. He shouted out our first record on the BBC at the end of 2018, which helped get us in gear for a second. Weren’t you on the same radio program with him when I saw you last in NYC?

不完全的。 Shearwater自2006年以来已经为Matador和Sub Pop做了七个专辑，我现在正在八分之一。至于eno，他混合了最后的赛道，“归巢”在洛马最近的专辑中，一支乐队，我开始了几年前与艾米莉交叉作为歌手。他于2018年底宣布我们在BBC上的第一次记录，这有助于让我们成为一秒钟。当我在纽约看到你时，我不是在与他同一广播节目吗？

Bri? We’re like the same person. We go shooting. There was a radio thing. Listen, though. Your book grows out of your work as an ornithologist, and your band is named after a bird, and I’ve seen you perform songs about birds. You did one here in Wilmington. What’s it all about?

Bri？我们就像同一个人。我们去拍摄。有一个收音机。但是听。您的书籍随着鸟类学家而脱离您的工作，您的乐队以一只鸟命名，我见过你对鸟类进行歌曲。你在威尔明顿做过一个。这完全是什么？

Paying close attention to birds draws me out of myself, which I crave more and more as I get older. Music does exactly the same thing—it gives you the feeling of being humbled and exalted at once.

密切关注鸟儿将我从自己身上吸引我，我渴望越来越多，因为我变老了。音乐完全相同 - 它会让你谦卑和尊重的感觉。
