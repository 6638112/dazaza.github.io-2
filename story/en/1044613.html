Hounded by Wildfires, Californians Rethink Their Willingness to Rebuild
加州人被野火困住，重新考虑他们重建的意愿

2021-01-17 04:47:02

The day Paradise burned, Aaron Singer was a skeptic. Then he saw the flames in his rearview mirror.

天堂被烧毁的那一天，亚伦·辛格（Aaron Singer）持怀疑态度。然后，他在后视镜中看到了火焰。

This was November 2018, and the Camp Fire, the most destructive wildfire in California history, was making swift and smoky headway through the Sierra Nevada foothills. It took fewer than four hours to rip through this town of 26,000 residents, reducing schools, businesses, and 11,000 homes into piles of smoldering ash. When Mr. Singer got the evacuation order, he believed it was another false alarm. But he peeled out of his driveway as flames licked his yard, making it out of town with minutes to spare.

那是2018年11月，营火是加利福尼亚历史上最具破坏性的野火，在内华达山脉的山麓上迅速而冒烟。花了不到四个小时的时间，这个小镇的26,000名居民就被撕裂了，减少了学校，企业和11,000座房屋的积灰。当辛格先生得到疏散命令时，他认为这是另一个错误的警报。但是，随着烈火舔到他的院子，他从车道上脱了下来，离开城镇只有几分钟的时间。

Paradise was lost. Eighty-five people died, and more than 90 percent of its population was driven out. Two years later, about 4,000 residents have returned to its scorched earth to lay new foundations and test fate once again. Mr. Singer, who has spent the past 48 months preparing to rebuild his home, is among them.

天堂迷失了。八十五人死亡，超过90％的人口被赶出去。两年后，大约4,000名居民回到了焦土，奠定了新的基础并再次考验了命运。辛格先生已经过去了48个月，准备重建自己的房屋。

For decades in this chaparral-covered ecosystem, it’s been a ritual: after the burn comes the rebuild. But as the nation’s most populous state stares down a seething climate crisis, one that cranked temperatures into triple digits last fall and set off a series of infernos that exploded, into bone dry air, the rebuilding process is beginning to look different.

在这个被丛林覆盖的生态系统中数十年来，这一直是一种仪式：烧毁之后进行重建。但是，随着该国人口最多的州开始注视着一场沸腾的气候危机，去年秋天该州的气温上升到三位数，并引发了一系列地狱爆炸，并变成了干燥的空气，重建过程看起来开始有所不同。

California has battled dual crises, with the largest wildfire season on record breaking out in the midst of the pandemic. At the close of 2020, as millions of Californians were put under a second lockdown in a bid to quell a massive second surge in Covid-19 cases, more than 4.2 million acres of the state had been scorched by nearly 10,000 fires. But in many ways, the crises have split the state into two: Northern California continues to reel from multiple megafires, including the August Complex Fire and the SCU Lightning Complex Fire that exploded in late summer. And in Southern California, Los Angeles is now the epicenter of the pandemic, leading the nation both in confirmed cases and in numbers of deaths.

加州与双重危机作斗争，在大流行期间爆发了有记录以来最大的野火季节。在2020年底，成千上万的加利福尼亚人受到第二次封锁，以平息Covid-19案的第二次大规模激增，该州超过420万英亩的土地被近10,000场大火烧焦。但是从许多方面来看，危机已将州一分为二：北加州继续遭受多次特大火灾的袭击，包括八月复杂大火和夏末爆炸的SCU闪电复杂大火。在南加州，洛杉矶现在是大流行的中心，在确诊病例和死亡人数上均领先全美。

In the midst of this, a historic housing shortage and low interest rates have pushed California’s home prices to record highs. In August, the median cost of a single family home in the state crossed $700,000; in September it climbed further.

在这种情况下，历史性的住房短缺和低利率促使加利福尼亚州的房价创下历史新高。八月份，该州单户住宅的平均价格超过了70万美元；九月份，它进一步攀升。

And while the cost of new homes is on the rise, many homeowners are finding the cost of rebuilding after a fire is even higher.

尽管新房的成本在上升，但许多房主发现火灾后的重建成本甚至更高。

Home rebuilds are on the decline across the entire state, triggered by a combination of contractor shortages, pressures on the rental market and an ever-escalating climate crisis that has become impossible to ignore. California, which remains mired in an affordable housing crisis, has seen new construction permits dwindle for the past two years after more than a decade of rebounding steadily after the 2008 recession. New home construction permits reached 120,000 in 2018, then dipped to 110,000 in 2019.

由于承包商短缺，租赁市场的压力以及不断升级的气候危机（已成为不可忽视的因素），整个州的房屋重建量正在下降。加州仍然陷入负担得起的住房危机之中，在经历了2008年经济衰退后的十多年稳步反弹之后，过去两年来新建筑许可证的数量减少了。 2018年，新房屋建设许可证达到120,000个，然后在2019年下降至110,000个。

The California Industry Research Board, which monitors construction and permit activity statewide, will publish its 2020 numbers in February but estimates that only 103,670 total housing units were issued last year.

加州工业研究委员会（California Industry Research Board）将在全州范围内监控建筑和许可活动，它将于2月发布2020年的数据，但估计去年仅发行了103,670套住房。

The board doesn’t track when permits are issued for fire-related losses. And “analyzing the effect of California’s wildfires on homebuilding has historically been difficult, as wildfires typically do not enclose themselves in one municipality alone,” wrote Marissa Saldivar, the board’s data journalist intern, in a recent report.

董事会不会跟踪何时发放与火灾有关的损失许可证。董事会数据记者实习生玛丽莎·萨尔迪瓦（Marissa Saldivar）在最近的一份报告中表示：“从历史上讲，分析加利福尼亚山火对房屋建筑的影响一直很困难，因为山火通常不会将自己包围在一个城市中。”

But the statewide decline in rebuilds, coupled with the increase and intensity in fires, points to a clear trend: Faced with the choice of rebuilding or starting afresh, more homeowners than ever before are choosing to cut their losses.

但是，全州范围内房屋重建的减少，加上火灾的增加和强度，表明了一个明显的趋势：面对重建或重新开始的选择，比以往任何时候都更多的房主选择减少损失。

A critical piece of the puzzle? The state is also short on contractors, which means homeowners looking to rebuild can find themselves in limbo for four or even five years.

一个关键的难题？该州还缺少承包商，这意味着寻求重建的房主可能会陷入困境甚至长达四年甚至五年。

“Even after the Camp Fire, you’d think we would have seen a spike in the number of permits, and yet we haven’t,” said Dan Dunmoyer, president and chief executive of the California Building Industry Association. “Most big insurance companies will just cut you a big check, and you can be sitting there looking at a check for $900,000. And you talk to contractors and they say: ‘Sure, I can build you a home, but I’m backed up for a year and a half.’ So we’re seeing a lot people just cut and run.”

加州建筑工业协会主席兼首席执行官丹·邓莫耶（Dan Dunmoyer）说：“即使在营火之后，您也会认为许可证数量会激增，但实际上我们还没有。” “大多数大型保险公司只会为您削减一张大额支票，您可以坐在那里查看90万美元的支票。然后，您与承包商交谈，他们说：“当然，我可以给您盖房子，但我得到了一年半的支持。”因此，我们看到很多人被砍掉了。”

There is one exception to this rule: rebuilds are holding steady where the land is particularly valuable, as it was in 2018 when the devastating Woolsey Fire tore through Malibu, one of Los Angeles’s most exclusive cliff-side hideaways. Three people were killed. The entire city was evacuated and 650 of its multimillion dollar homes, including those belonging to Miley Cyrus, Gerard Butler and Robin Thicke, were vanquished; but today more than one-third of those homes already have rebuild permits (in Paradise, the number stands at just over 10 percent).

该规则有一个例外：在土地特别珍贵的地方，重建工作保持稳定，就像2018年毁灭性的伍尔西大火穿过马里布（Malibu）撕毁一样，马里布是洛杉矶最独特的悬崖边度假胜地之一。三人被杀。整个城市被疏散，数百万美元房屋中的650座被撤离，其中包括麦莉·赛勒斯，杰拉德·巴特勒和罗宾·赛克的房屋。但是今天，这些房屋中有超过三分之一的房屋已经获得了重建许可（在天堂，这个数字刚刚超过10％）。

“Rebuilding after a fire is sort of like someone who gets a shark bite and still goes back and surfs,” said Michael Nourmand, president of the Los Angeles brokerage Nourmand& Associates. “But people in L.A. have a short memory. Most people are planning to rebuild.”

It’s always easier to indulge an urge to rebuild, however, if you have deep pockets. California in 2008 enforced strict fire-safe building codes, which require reinforced roofs made of brick, masonry or concrete; walls that can withstand high temperatures and landscaping that is free of highly flammable flora. In 2011, the state also required all single-family homes to be equipped with sprinkler systems. These codes can increase costs by as much as $20,000 or more for homeowners looking to rebuild an older, pre-2008 home.

但是，如果您有足够的资金，放纵重建的欲望总是比较容易的。加利福尼亚州在2008年实施了严格的防火建筑法规，要求使用砖，砖石或混凝土加固屋顶；可以承受高温和美化环境的墙壁，没有高度易燃的植物。 2011年，纽约州还要求所有单户住宅都必须配备洒水系统。对于希望重建2008年前的旧房子的房主而言，这些法规可能使成本增加多达20,000美元或更多。

Homeowners who do choose to rebuild must enter the rental market while waiting it out, putting pressure on a housing system where prices are already inflated and demand far outstrips supply. And the state’s most vulnerable residents inevitably suffer the domino effects. California’s homelessness numbers rose 16 percent in 2019 and have now swelled to more than 150,000 of its residents. Because of Covid-19, those numbers are expected to get worse.

确实选择重建的房主必须在等待期间进入租赁市场，这给房价已经高涨且需求远远超过供应的住房体系带来了压力。该州最脆弱的居民不可避免地会遭受多米诺骨牌效应。加州的无家可归人数在2019年增长了16％，现在已经膨胀到超过15万的居民。由于Covid-19，预计这些数字会更糟。

“When thousands of homes burn down, those people are pushed elsewhere, which pushes rental prices up. That means affordable housing also goes up and housing insecurity goes by the wayside,” said James Ryan, president of Time for Homes, a nonprofit dedicated to eliminating chronic homelessness. Gov. Gavin Newsom’s eviction moratorium, meant to protect tenants from losing their homes because of hardships experienced from the Covid-19 pandemic, expires Feb. 1. “当成千上万的房屋烧毁时，这些人被推到其他地方，这推高了租金价格。这意味着负担得起的住房也将增加，住房的不安全感也将消退。”致力于消除长期无家可归现象的非营利组织Time for Homes总裁James Ryan说。州长加文·纽瑟姆（Gavin Newsom）的驱逐暂停令旨在保护租户免于因Covid-19大流行所遭受的苦难而失去家园，该禁令将于2月1日到期。

“There are always people who get lost in the shuffle,” Mr. Ryan said. “There are forced evictions, and those people will be looking for other housing, just as people who have lost homes from fire will be looking for temporary housing too. The crunch is causing a population shift to California’s more rural enclaves. In tiny El Dorado County, east of Sacramento, sales in 2020 were up 28 percent (compared to a small decline in Sacramento).

紧缩导致人口向加州更多的农村飞地转移。在萨克拉曼多以东的小埃尔多拉多县（El Dorado County），2020年的销售额增长了28％（萨克拉曼多则略有下降）。

But it’s the Inland Empire, the swath of Riverside and San Bernardino Counties that sits inland and adjacent to Los Angeles, that is the fastest-growing sector of the state, and it’s largely been spared the megafires that have raged through many of the state’s major metropolitan areas, including San Diego, Los Angeles, and the San Francisco Bay Area, over the last two decades. Covid-19, which has prompted an upsizing, exurban surge has only increased this trend.

But moving away from fire risk isn’t a viable option for everyone, especially not in a state already facing an extreme housing shortfall.

但是，对于所有人来说，摆脱火灾风险并不是一个可行的选择，尤其是在已经面临严重住房短缺的州。

Since the Camp Fire, Mr. Singer and his wife, Shannon, have been renting an apartment in Chico, about 20 miles away, while navigating the various headaches — insurance, zoning, construction, planning — required to rebuild their home. They have also started a nonprofit, Paradise Stronger, which utilizes their background in fitness coaching to bring mental health care to residents coping with trauma from the disaster. At first, they were committed to being part of Paradise’s ambitious recovery plan to rebuild the entire city from scratch, which includes more parks and green space, fire-safe landscaping and improved evacuation routes and warning systems.

自营火以来，辛格先生和他的妻子香农（Shannon）一直在约20英里外的奇科（Chico）租一间公寓，同时解决了重建房屋所需的各种麻烦—保险，分区，建筑和规划。他们还成立了一家非营利性组织“天堂更强者”（Paradise Stronger），利用他们在健身教练方面的背景，为应对灾难带来的创伤的居民提供心理保健。最初，他们致力于成为Paradise雄心勃勃的恢复计划的一部分，以从头开始重建整个城市，其中包括更多的公园和绿地，防火安全的美化环境以及改善的疏散路线和预警系统。

许多保险公司也放弃了被认为风险过高的地区的保单：10月，加州保险部报告称，2019年全州范围内房屋保险公司拒绝续签保单的比例上升了31％，邮政编码区域的这一比例跃升至61％火灾风险较高。

A handful of new developments have responded instead by incorporating new resilience protocols into their building strategies in areas well acquainted with wildfire. In Southern California, Rancho Mission Viejo, which is in the southern part of Orange County and is the largest new community in the state, has been in development since 2001 on 23,000 acres of open space. When completed, it will provide 14,000 homes (including 6,000 homes for 55-plus residents), and its development plans stipulate that seventy-five percent of that open space be preserved.

相反，一些新的开发项目通过将新的恢复协议纳入其熟悉野火地区的建筑策略中来做出回应。在南加州，Rancho Mission Viejo位于奥兰治县的南部，是该州最大的新社区，自2001年以来就在开发中，占地23,000英亩。建成后，它将提供14,000套住房（其中包括6,000套住房供55岁以上的居民使用），其发展计划规定，应保留该开放空间的百分之七十五。

Fire resilience is a tenet of the community’s construction, and they implemented many wildfire resistance tactics long before they became state and local requirements. The community was recently highlighted in a report by the Urban Land Institute (as was the entire town of Paradise, whose rebuild, the report said, could serve as a template for other wildfire-affected communities).

弹性是社区建设的宗旨，他们早在成为州和地方要求之前就实施了许多防野火策略。城市土地研究所（Urban Land Institute）最近在一份报告中强调了该社区（报告称，整个天堂镇也是如此，其重建可以作为其他受野火影响的社区的模板）。

Each neighborhood at Rancho Mission Viejo is surrounded by a 110-foot-wide fuel modification zone — a strip of land where combustible vegetation has been replaced with fire-resistant plants. Certain plant species are prohibited. Construction materials are noncombustible; automatic fire sprinklers were mandatory in every home well before the California law changed in 2011. And the added cost is now negligible; when construction began, those fire-safe modifications were adding between $4,000 and $10,000 to the bottom line of each home to a comparable-sized new home in the area, but have since dipped to as low as $1,000. Jay Bullock, Rancho Mission Viejo’s vice president for planning and entitlement, said that was because that fire-safe methods, once considered radical, have become more commonplace.

Rancho Mission Viejo的每个社区都被一个110英尺宽的燃料改良区所包围，这片土地上的可燃植被已被耐火植物取代。禁止某些植物种类。建筑材料不可燃；在2011年加利福尼亚州法律修改之前，每个家庭都必须使用自动喷水灭火器。现在，增加的成本可以忽略不计；当建筑开始时，这些防火安全的修改使该地区同等规模的新房屋的每座房屋的底线增加了4,000美元至10,000美元，但此后已降至1000美元。 Rancho Mission Viejo负责计划和授权的副总裁Jay Bullock说，这是因为曾经被认为是激进的消防安全方法已经变得越来越普遍。

And in a state where livable space is at a premium, building safely in high fire hazard zones is the key to a sustainable future, said Mike Balsamo, Rancho Mission Viejo’s senior vice president for governmental relations.

Rancho Mission Viejo政府关系高级副总裁迈克•巴尔萨莫（Mike Balsamo）表示，在宜居空间十分珍贵的州，在高火险区安全建筑是实现可持续未来的关键。

“There is a playbook,” he said. “We can create the most fire-safe community possible.”

他说：“有一本剧本。” “我们可以创建最安全的社区。”
