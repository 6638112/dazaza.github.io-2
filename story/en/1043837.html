'Exercised' Explains Why It Can Be Hard to Commit to Working Out
“锻炼”解释了为什么很难致力于锻炼

2021-01-10 13:18:26

If you live in the U.S. and can claim more than 4,774 steps daily, you're exceeding the average American's total. Comparable figures from England and Japan are 5,444 and 6,010 daily steps, respectively — with this info derived from cellphone data.

如果您居住在美国，并且每天可以要求超过4,774步，那么您将超过美国人的平均总数。来自英国和日本的可比数字分别为每天5,444和6,010步-该信息来自手机数据。

The distance that individuals walk daily varies, with a host of factors affecting the total, including where you live, what work you do and whether you are able to walk at all. In a riveting book published Tuesday, Exercised: Why Something We Never Evolved to Do is Healthy and Rewarding, Harvard University evolutionary biologist Daniel E. Lieberman cites the step data to make a point. Many of us today are fairly inactive (under 5,000 steps is defined as "sedentary"), so much so that our activity levels are mismatched with the way we evolved.

个人每天行走的距离各不相同，其中有许多因素会影响整体行走，包括您的住所，从事的工作以及是否能够行走。哈佛大学进化生物学家丹尼尔·E·利伯曼（Daniel E.Lieberman）在星期二出版的一本铆钉书《锻炼：为什么我们从来没有做过的事情是健康和奖励》中引用了逐步数据来阐明这一点。今天，我们许多人都相当不活跃（5,000步以下被定义为“基本”），以至于我们的活动水平与进化方式不匹配。

Exercise can be understood as "discretionary, planned physical activity for the sake of physical improvement." In other words, when we work out, we set aside time to exercise and do it because we have goals related to better bodies and better health.

运动可以理解为为了身体改善而进行的有计划的有计划的体育活动。换句话说，当我们锻炼时，我们会留出时间进行锻炼，因为我们的目标与更好的身体和更好的健康有关。

Our ancestors never did any such thing. Certainly, they moved a lot in the course of their daily lives. We know this because the hunter-gatherer lifestyle — making a living off the land, without farming — demands it, and also because data from contemporary hunter-gatherers like the Hadza Indigenous people of Tanzania give us some clues to past activity patterns. Hadza individuals are about 12 times more active than the average American or European. Elderly people remain highly active, as they must have in the past. "Almost no one in the Stone Age," Lieberman notes, "least of all grandparents, managed to avoid hours of walking, running, digging, climbing, and other manual labors."

我们的祖先从来没有做过这样的事情。当然，他们在日常生活中移动了很多。我们之所以知道这一点，是因为猎人-采集者的生活方式-在不耕种的情况下在土地上谋生，并且因为当代猎人-采集者（例如坦桑尼亚的Hadza土著人）的数据为我们提供了过去活动方式的一些线索。哈扎族人的活跃度是普通美国人或欧洲人的12倍左右。老年人必须像过去一样保持高度活跃。 ＆＃34;石器时代几乎没有人，＆＃34;利伯曼（Lieberman）指出，与所有祖父母中的最少一样，他们避免了数小时的步行，奔跑，挖掘，攀爬和其他体力劳动。

Yet, here's the central point: There was no exercise in the Stone Age! People had high activity levels simply because that's how it was possible to survive by hunting and gathering.

然而，这是中心点：石器时代没有运动！人们之所以拥有较高的活动水平，仅仅是因为这是通过狩猎和采集生存的方式。

With that distinction in place, Lieberman begins a process of myth-busting about exercise. Some exercise enthusiasts, he writes, insist that "we were born to exercise because for millions of years our hunter-gatherer ancestors survived through walking, running, climbing, and other physical activities." On the contrary — and here comes a surprising but scientifically sensible statement — we evolved to limit our physical activity whenever we reasonably could. With all the survival tasks requiring our energy, the last thing needed ancestrally was to set aside time to work out.

有了这样的区别，利伯曼就开始了关于运动的神话破坏过程。他写道，一些运动爱好者坚持认为我们出生是为了运动，因为我们的狩猎采集者祖先经过步行，跑步，爬山和其他体育活动而生存了数百万年。相反，这是一个令人惊讶但科学合理的说法，我们在合理的可能范围内逐渐限制身体活动。在所有生存任务都需要我们的精力的情况下，祖先需要做的最后一件事就是预留时间锻炼身体。

An irresistible aspect of Exercised is Lieberman's firm stance that no shame or stigma be attached to those who find it challenging to sustain an exercise program: "So if, as you read these words, you are seated in a chair or lounging in bed and feeling guilty about your indolence, take solace in knowing that your current state of physical inactivity is an ancient, fundamental strategy to allocate scarce energy sensibly."

锻炼身体不可阻挡的一面是利伯曼（Lieberman）坚定的立场，即对那些认为维持锻炼程序具有挑战性的人不要感到羞耻或羞辱：因此，如果您在阅读这些文字时坐在一个坐在椅子上或懒洋洋地躺在床上，对自己的懒惰感到内，请宽慰自己，知道当前身体不活动的状态是一种古老的基本策略，可以合理地分配稀缺的能量。＆＃34;

Indolence is not good for us, of course. Sitting for long hours, for instance, encourages inflammation throughout the body and is associated with chronic disease. This is especially true of the way we tend to sit in our culture — on a chair — compared to cultural traditions of squatting, kneeling or sitting on the ground, which causes the muscles to be more highly active. Yet if you're attached to your chair or couch, all is not lost: In one study, in which participants interrupted their sitting with just 100 seconds of movement every half-hour, the result was lower blood levels of sugar, fat and bad cholesterol.

当然，懒惰对我们不利。例如，长时间坐着会促进全身炎症，并与慢性疾病有关。与蹲，跪或坐在地上的文化传统相比，我们倾向于坐在椅子上的文化中尤其如此，这导致肌肉更加活跃。但是，如果您将自己固定在椅子或沙发上，一切都不会丢失：在一项研究中，参与者每半小时仅运动100秒就中断了坐姿，结果是血糖，脂肪和血脂降低了。和坏胆固醇。

Another exceptionally informative part of the book discusses the damage-and-repair cycle brought on by exercise. Lieberman explains more clearly than I've ever read what exercise does to the body and how the body then begins to repair itself afterwards. Briefly put, exercise unleashes a flood of waste products and unstable oxygen molecules that compromise cells' function. Following a workout, the body slowly begins to repair the damage, through a suite of anti-inflammatory and cleanup responses. Often, this repair process works so well that it leads to a state of better health in the body overall.

该书的另一个特别有用的部分讨论了运动带来的损害和修复周期。利伯曼（Lieberman）向我解释得比我以前阅读过的运动更清楚，运动对身体有什么作用，然后身体如何开始自我修复。简而言之，运动会释放出大量的废物和不稳定的氧分子，从而损害细胞。功能。锻炼后，身体会通过一系列抗炎和清洁反应，慢慢开始修复损伤。通常，这种修复过程效果很好，可导致整个身体健康。

This beneficial process evolved in an evolutionary context of physical activity, as carried out by our ancestors. Thus, repair today does not happen efficiently if you are sedentary: "We never evolved to activate these maintenance and repair responses as effectively in the absence of regularly physical activity." Additionally, an increase in BDNF (brain-derived neurotrophic factor) that helps protect our brains against Alzheimer's disease increases with physical effort, for the same evolutionary reason. Insights like these alone make reading the book worthwhile.

正如我们的祖先所进行的，这种有益的过程是在体育活动的进化背景下发展起来的。因此，如果久坐不动，今天的修理将无法有效进行：＆＃34;在没有规律的体育锻炼的情况下，我们从来没有像现在这样有效地激活这些维护和修理响应。此外，出于相同的进化原因，有助于身体抵抗阿尔茨海默氏病的BDNF（脑源性神经营养因子）的增加也随着身体的努力而增加。仅凭这些见解，便值得阅读。

To integrate exercise into our lives in sustained fun ways is a goal we should all have, Lieberman says, though he appropriately explains that certain disabilities may make this difficult or impossible. We can bring this about by exercising socially, for example; our species is gregarious by nature and many people enjoy collective activity, plus, if we commit to an exercise meetup, we're less likely to succumb to that urge not to exercise. (The book was mostly written before the COVID-19 pandemic forced temporary suspension of shared exercise activities in the name of good public health.)

利伯曼说，以持续有趣的方式将运动融入我们的生活是我们所有人应该实现的目标，尽管他适当地解释了某些残疾可能会使这种困难或不可能发生。例如，我们可以通过社交活动来实现这一目标。我们的物种天生就是群居动物，许多人都喜欢集体活动，而且，如果我们参加运动聚会，我们就不太可能屈服于不运动的冲动。 （这本书主要是在COVID-19大流行之前以良好公共卫生的名义强行暂时停止共享锻炼活动的。）

The oft-cited recommendation to exercise 150 minutes a week Lieberman doesn't discount, but he emphasizes there's no optimal routine of exercise type or quantity because we all have different bodies and circumstances. A good across-the-board summary is this: "Make exercise necessary and fun. Do mostly cardio, but also some weights. Some is better than none. Keep it up as you age."

Lieberman经常建议每周进行150分钟的锻炼，但他强调，由于我们每个人的身体和情况都不尽相同，因此没有最佳的锻炼方式或运动量。一个很好的全面总结是：＆＃34;使锻炼既必要又有趣。主要做有氧运动，但也要做一些举重。有些总比没有好。随着年龄的增长，保持健康。＆＃34;

Exercised is written in a warm, sometimes dryly amusing tone that's highly appealing. Colorful personal stories enliven the book. Follow along as Lieberman accompanies two Hadza men on a hunt, rides a dog sled across icy terrain Greenland and tries to outrun a horse in a race in Arizona.

锻炼时以温暖，有时令人愉悦的语调写成，极具吸引力。丰富多彩的个人故事使这本书更加生动。随后，利伯曼（Lieberman）陪同两名哈扎（Hadza）男子进行狩猎，骑着狗拉雪橇穿越冰冷的格陵兰（Greenland），并试图在亚利桑那州的一场比赛中超越一匹马。

Lieberman makes a superb guide for anyone wishing to understand why it can be hard to commit to exercising and why we should do it anyway.

利伯曼（Lieberman）为希望了解为什么很难坚持锻炼以及为什么我们还是应该这样做的人提供了极好的指南。
