The Best Way to Learn Online? Be a Lurker

Lately I have been trying to get through the UN Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change's big report, the one that came out late last year, called “ Climate Change 2021: The Physical Science Basis.” It's a challenge because (a) I must, um, learn as I go, and (b) the PDF is nearly 4,000 pages of aggregated, footnoted, illustrated scientific consensus about weather. That's too much consensus; everyone agrees. Yet obviously they—the hundreds of IPCC-affiliated scientists who compiled this behemoth— want us to read it, right? The thing exists, so I should at least try. Plus it's free to download. I love a bargain.

However, the gigantic PDF broke my e-reader. In time I got it to work, but the font was too small. So I used a command-line program called mutool to split each page in half, and now I can read the book in landscape mode with bigger type, a little hack of which I am embarrassingly proud—although the resulting document is nearly 8,000 pages long.

然而，巨大的PDF破坏了我的电子阅读器。我终于把它用上了，但字体太小了。因此，我使用一个名为mutool的命令行程序将每一页一分为二，现在我可以用更大的字体在横向模式下阅读这本书，这是一个让我感到尴尬的小技巧，尽管生成的文档将近8000页长。

Eight kilopages is a lot, but a very long book is still a book: You start at the beginning, skip the intro pages, skim over the “Summary for Policymakers,” jump forward 7,000 pages or so and read a little from “Annex V: Monsoons.” OK, so maybe it's not really a book, more of a database that results from a complicated bureaucratic process. I make it a few pages at a time, until my brain refuses to go any further without a Twitter break.

8千页是很多，但一本很长的书仍然是一本书：你从开头开始，跳过介绍页，浏览“政策制定者概要”，向前跳7000页左右，读一点“附件五：季风”好吧，也许是'；这其实不是一本书，更像是一个复杂的官僚程序产生的数据库。我一次只写几页，直到我的大脑在没有Twitter休息的情况下拒绝再继续写下去。

Frustrated by my slow progress, I started to use Twitter to look up the climate scientists themselves, click through their timelines. It's a good way to see what they're about—to pick up social signals that might bring the rest of their network into better focus. As you'd expect from a group of extremely smart literal cloud people, they aren't exactly dank memelord true posters. Some speak in press releases; some like to share science news. Yet personal details always leak out—kids and spouses and the like. You see their little avatar faces, come across some artifact like a Spotify playlist, witness their naked enthusiasm for glaciers. Reading through this stuff, I definitely feel like I'm creeping.

由于进度缓慢，我感到沮丧，于是开始使用Twitter来查找气候科学家自己，点击他们的时间表。它'；这是一个很好的方式来了解他们'；We’我们即将获得社会信号，这些信号可能会让他们的人际网络中的其他人更加关注。作为你'；我期待一群非常聪明的云计算人，他们不是'；梅梅洛德的海报真的很潮湿。一些人在新闻稿中发言；有些人喜欢分享科学新闻。然而，个人细节总是泄露给孩子和配偶之类的人。你可以看到他们小小的头像脸，看到Spotify播放列表之类的人工制品，见证他们对冰川赤裸裸的热情。通读这些东西，我肯定觉得我'；我在爬行。

I think this sort of filthy spyhood—peepreading—is a particularly internettish way of learning. Like when you wake up and all the social media posts circulating in your peer group are suddenly about a thing, but you don't know what the thing is. Most of the time the tweets are elliptical, like “Couldn't happen to a nicer columnist” or “Just shows you what actually goes on in the mineralogy community.” In fact, the vaguer they are, the more likely the thing is to be really bad and/or salacious. So now you have to—no higher priority—blow up your morning and use context clues to unpack the scandal, poking around the network, seeing who liked what, pasting tweets into your group chats to see if anyone has any insight, until you find out who has been called out for what by a now-locked account, with the end result being, of course, that you do figure it out, some squalid nasty thing someone said or did, and, welp, you can go about your day and wait for the story to show up in an essay about cancel culture.

我认为这种肮脏的间谍窥视是一种特别的互联网学习方式。比如，当你醒来时，你的同龄人群中流传的所有社交媒体帖子突然都在谈论一件事，但你没有'；我不知道这是什么。大多数时候，推文都是椭圆形的，比如“不可能发生在更好的专栏作家身上”或“只是向你展示矿物学社区的实际情况”事实上，他们越含糊，事情就越有可能变得非常糟糕和/或淫秽。因此，现在你不必再把早上的事情搞砸了，利用上下文线索来解开丑闻，在网络上四处打探，看看谁喜欢什么，把推文粘贴到你的群聊中，看看有没有人有什么见解，直到你发现谁被一个现已锁定的帐户要求做什么，最终结果当然是，你确实明白了，有人说了或做了一些肮脏肮脏的事情，好吧，你可以继续你的一天，等待这个故事出现在一篇关于文化的文章中。

I'm not proud of this. I just do it. Sometimes it's the only way to learn about subcultures and communities. When you're researching software history, which is something I like to do, you have to venture into copyright-dubious Discords and peculiar archive-focused websites and lurk around until you find out how to get the good stuff. You have to page through a bunch of gossip, drama, fights over whether emulating old computers in software is “real” or not. And if you lurk long enough, read enough forum threads, someone will post a temporary link to the special Google doc filled with links to confusingly titled zip files. The internet turns the world into a puzzle you must solve to feel safe. It's scary, of course, but when you solve the puzzle you feel an unbelievable sense of control. (This is also, alas, how you get QAnon.)

我'；我并不为此感到骄傲。我就这么做。有时是'；这是了解亚文化和社区的唯一途径。当你'；重新研究软件历史，这是我喜欢做的事情，你必须冒险进入版权可疑的不和谐和特殊的以归档为中心的网站，并潜伏在周围，直到你发现如何获得好的东西。你必须翻阅一大堆流言蜚语、戏剧、关于在软件中模仿旧电脑是否“真实”的争论。如果你潜伏的时间足够长，阅读了足够多的论坛帖子，有人会发布一个临时链接到特殊的谷歌文档，里面充满了标题混乱的zip文件的链接。互联网把世界变成了一个谜，你必须解决它才能感到安全。它'；当然，这很可怕，但当你解开谜题时，你会有一种难以置信的控制感。（唉，这也是你得到卡农的原因。）

But being a sneakbrowser is also just who I am. Owing to cowardice, extraordinary shyness, or perhaps toxic masculinity (choose whichever works for you), I'd rather spy for a year than ask a direct question. This is an unfortunate quality in a journalist. The job of a journalist is to call smart people and ask them to explain things. Those people have to talk to you because it helps their careers. I find that I always slightly misunderstand what they tell me; I get the tone wrong, and then they send corrective emails that pulp my soul. I do better looking at the artifacts of a civilization than participating in it. Twitter, newsletters, Slacks, Discords: Lurking is learning.

但我也是一个偷偷摸摸的浏览者。由于怯懦，特别害羞，或者可能是有毒的男性气质（选择适合你的），我'；我宁愿做一年的间谍，也不愿直接问问题。这是记者的不幸品质。记者的工作是给聪明人打电话，让他们解释事情。这些人必须和你谈谈，因为这有助于他们的事业。我发现我总是稍微误解他们对我说的话；我弄错了语调，然后他们发送纠正邮件，玷污了我的灵魂。我更喜欢看一个文明的文物，而不是参与其中。推特、时事通讯、休闲裤、不和谐：潜伏就是学习。

So give me a network and the tools to read it. Give me the textbook called Web Markup Languages Produced in Anger, the anthology of GitHub messages called You Can't Program in Lisp Because You're an Idiot, the climate report called “Energy-Systems People and Earth-Systems People: The Longest Thread.”

所以给我一个网络和阅读它的工具。给我一本叫做《愤怒中产生的网络标记语言》的教科书，一本叫做《你可以'》的GitHub信息文集；因为你'；你是个白痴，气候报告称之为“能源系统人和地球系统人：最长的线索”

There's a software truism, credited to former Microsoft Windows head Steven Sinofsky, that says “Don't ship your org chart.” In other words, build products (or websites) for users; don't make them figure out which department in your organization does what. But everyone ships their org chart. And thank God, because if I want to understand an organization, sometimes I spider its entire website using a command-line tool called wget. You can tell what makes a place tick just from the folder structure—how many blog posts, how much news it produces, whether it sorts by day or month, the sheer number of folders, all the different corners of the operation. Does the design change between departments? Is there a charismatic leader who shows up everywhere? What web host are they using? (The IPCC website, for its part, is about 15 gigs, has PDFs going back decades, uses WordPress, and has caching turned on—all signs of health.) Please, world: Keep shipping your org chart.

那里'；这是微软Windows前负责人史蒂文·辛诺夫斯基（Steven Sinofsky）提出的一个软件真理，即“不要发布组织结构图”换句话说，为用户建立产品（或网站）；唐'；Don’不要让他们搞清楚你公司的哪个部门做什么。但每个人都会发布他们的组织结构图。感谢上帝，因为如果我想了解一个组织，有时我会使用一个名为wget的命令行工具浏览它的整个网站。你可以从文件夹结构、博客帖子数量、新闻发布量、按天还是按月排序、文件夹数量、操作的所有不同方面来判断是什么让一个地方运转良好。各部门之间的设计是否有变化？有没有一个魅力四射的领导者随处可见？他们使用的是什么网络主机？（就IPCC网站而言，其容量约为15千兆，PDF可追溯到几十年前，使用WordPress，并开启了所有健康迹象的缓存。）请继续发送你的组织结构图。

Now, look. I do come by some of my climate knowledge legitimately. I ask questions. I attend Zoom seminars and learn about REMO climate models. I've read multiple textbooks (well, sections of them). But the information so rarely sticks that way. I feel like the internet has made me immune to coherent narrative. So while I thought I was going to read the IPCC PDF and learn about the planet, of course I can't do that. I've turned into a paranoid basement detective, using digital string and note cards to figure out how the world works.

When people ask me what I am reading these days, I usually fish around for a credible answer, but the truth is “this one mailing list from 1999” or “a really good GitHub commit thread that blew up terribly.” I learn by browsing until I forget what I didn't understand. This method can be frustrating to witness, and it has not led me to be the most deadline-driven of mammals, but it's a fantastic hobby. Maybe one day I'll make it 100 pages into “Climate Change 2021: The Physical Science Basis.” But by the time you read this, IPCC Working Group II may have released its report on “climate change impacts, adaptation and vulnerability”—i.e., the bad, scary stuff. This document will also, according to the outline agreed upon in 2017, be many kilopages. Sadly, I'm looking forward to it.
